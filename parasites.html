Parasites

What Are Parasites?

Parasites are ocean animals that live on or inside other animals, called hosts. Instead of hunting or grazing, parasites get their nutrients directly from their host’s body. This relationship can weaken the host, but parasites play a natural role in regulating populations and maintaining biodiversity in marine ecosystems.

How Parasites Feed

Marine parasites attach to or enter their host and feed on blood, skin, tissue, or bodily fluids. Some remain attached for long periods, while others feed quickly and move on. Many parasites have specialized mouthparts or hooks that help them stay attached.

Examples of Marine Parasites

  • Lampreys: Use suction-cup mouths and sharp teeth to feed on the blood of fish.
  • Copepods: Tiny crustaceans that attach to fish and feed on skin or mucus.
  • Isopods: Some species replace the tongues of fish and feed on their blood.
  • Marine Leeches: Attach to fish, turtles, and marine mammals.

Why Parasites Matter

Although parasites may seem harmful, they are essential to healthy ecosystems. They help control host populations, remove weak or sick individuals, and influence predator-prey relationships. Parasites also provide food for other animals, such as fish and crustaceans.

Role in the Food Web

Parasites occupy a unique position in the food web. They feed on hosts but are also eaten by predators. This makes them part of both the consumer and prey levels of the ecosystem. Their presence helps maintain balance and diversity.

Adaptations of Parasites

Many parasites have evolved specialized adaptations such as hooks, suction structures, and flattened bodies that help them attach to hosts. Some can even alter the behavior of their hosts to increase their own survival.
